Introduction and Background

We used data provided by the Office of the State Treasurer of California for the California Tax Credit (CATC) program, by the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD)  for the Low-Income Housing Tax Credit (LIHTC) database and by ProPublica through the Nonprofit Explorer database.

One of the issues with finding data is nonprofits do not need to disaggregate funding all the time so we cannot determine the exact flow of money through their filings. The more robust and transparent method is through grant funders who provide the money to nonprofits. But the same issue is raised with private non-profit funding sources: they do not need to disaggregate where they are sending money to the extent we can use data to understand it. Thus, we can only use datasets that are public such as governmental records and filings which don’t contain that information.

For Assignment 1, we declared we want to look at the efficiency of non-profit housing developers when building affordable housing. We will use data on types of housing as well as comparisons to for-profit developers to understand how these non-profit housing developers help their communities.

To incentivize housing developers to build affordable housing, which is less profitable than other types of housing due to regulated rents, the state of California and HUD provide funding in the form of tax credits. After a location with affordable housing is “Placed In Service” (PIS), it is usable as housing and the developer is given tax credits (Dunn, 2020, p. 2; Keightley, 2023, p. 6). Since it is expensive to build, developers often sell off their credits in exchange for financing for building (Keightley, 2023, p. 4). The PIS year is the same as the year the tax credits are released so we will use that data to understand how much the governmental bodies budget for housing each year.

Since the number of non-profit organizations is vastly outnumbered by the number of for-profit firms, many analyses must be made as a proportion. The chart below should not be counted as one of our six charts but is here for context.

New Construction Compared to Acquisition and Rehabilitation

When developers choose to build a new building outright rather than buying and updating an existing building, the costs for developing and maintaining associated with each unit increase by about 25% to 45% ($40,000 to $71,000) over their lifetime (Wilkins, 2015). While it may not always be feasible to build a new building or rehabilitate an existing structure, the projects non-profit and for-profit developers choose can signal their priorities in how efficient they want to be. The years were chosen because they were recent enough to not be affected by factors such as changing attitudes towards construction and complete data.

Fig 5. For-profit housing developers build more new construction projects than non-profit developers almost every year in our time span. This may be due to religious institutions opening their land up for affordable housing (Bandlamudi, 2022; Nguyen, 2023). Both groups  have increased new construction projects which is worrying as new construction is worse for the environment and costs more (Alba-Rodríguez et al., 2017; Wilkins et al., 2015). The decrease of efficiency of funding per unit may be connected to and driven by this trend. Data from LIHTC, CATC and ProPublica.

Targeted Housing for Vulnerable Populations

Populations who require affordable housing are very diverse and have many different needs. The dataset includes “targeted” housing, properties specially built for large families, seniors, those with special needs, and other “At-Risk” categories. These types of units are more expensive than standard and single room units to build with large family units adding an additional 31% (around $89,000)  and senior units adding 13% (around $52,000) on average to the cost in California (Newman, 2014, p. 7). Building more of these “target” projects may be more expensive in costs but can better serve communities that need it the most.

Fig 6. Non-profit and for-profit organizations seem to be building similar proportions of targeted projects. As the population becomes more elderly and the housing affordability crisis increases, there may be more need for such housing, driving costs per unit up as targeted housing increases in demand (Ludden, 2024; Vasquez, 2023).  Data from LIHTC, CATC and ProPublica.

Federal Award Comparison

While there are many factors driving the costs up overall, for-profits and non-profits operate very similarly when it comes to building. There is a difference in how much funding is awarded to each type of organization.

Fig 7. While the average funding awarded to developers from the federal government is very similar, there has been a recent trend of for-profit developers pocketing up to half a million dollars more. Non-profit housing developers may need to be more aggressive to capture more funding to cover the rising costs of per unit construction. Data from LIHTC, CATC and ProPublica.
